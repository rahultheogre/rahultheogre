Problem

Manual media monitoring doesn't scale — and keyword search misses what matters.

Clients needed to track how their brands, executives, and competitors were being covered across hundreds of publications daily. The existing approach — human analysts scanning clippings — couldn't keep pace with volume, and keyword-based tools flagged mentions without understanding context, tone, or which specific entities the sentiment applied to.

The core challenge was that a single article could carry positive sentiment about one company and negative sentiment about another mentioned in the same paragraph. Any system that scored at the article level was nearly useless for actionable brand intelligence. The platform needed entity-level understanding, not document-level aggregation.

Architecture

Shared Encoder, Four Inference Heads

STAGE 01

Ingestion & Preprocessing

Batch article fetch from publisher feeds. Deduplication via content hash. Sentence segmentation, entity span detection, normalisation before encoding.

STAGE 02

BERT Encoding

Single forward pass produces contextual embeddings for the full article and each entity span. Shared representation feeds all downstream heads — no redundant computation.

STAGE 03

Multi-task Inference

Four parallel heads: NER (token classification), entity sentiment (span-level), abstractive summarisation (seq2seq decoder), BERTopic topic assignment (c-TF-IDF on BERT clusters).

94% NER F1
STAGE 04

Structured Output

Results serialised to JSON: per-entity sentiment scores, NER tags, summary string, topic IDs. FastAPI serves downstream dashboards and client integrations.

Design Decisions

Why these choices, not others.

WHY BERT OVER CLASSICAL NLP (SPACY / VADER)?

Media language is context-dependent — the same word means different things in different sentences

Classical NLP pipelines use static word vectors and rule-based sentiment lexicons. They fail on media text where "volatile" describing a CEO's strategy is negative, but "volatile" describing market conditions driving a company's growth is neutral-to-positive. BERT's bidirectional context window captures these distinctions. Entity-level sentiment especially requires understanding what the surrounding clauses attribute to a specific span — a problem classical approaches cannot solve reliably.

// Rejected: spaCy + VADER pipeline (no contextual entity-sentiment), TextBlob (lexicon-based, ignores negation context)
WHY MULTI-TASK (SHARED ENCODER) OVER SEPARATE MODELS?

One BERT forward pass is cheaper than four — and joint training improves all heads

Running four independent BERT models at inference means four full forward passes per article. A shared encoder computes representations once and feeds all task heads in parallel. Beyond compute efficiency, multi-task training provides regularisation — the NER head and sentiment head share gradients, which improves entity boundary detection because the model learns that entity spans are also units of sentiment attribution. The heads reinforce each other.

// Rejected: four independent fine-tuned BERT models (4x inference cost, no cross-task signal), pipeline chaining (error propagation from NER into sentiment)
WHY BERTOPIC OVER LDA?

LDA requires fixing K upfront and produces incoherent topics on short media texts

Latent Dirichlet Allocation assumes a fixed number of topics chosen before training, which requires manual tuning and breaks when the topic landscape shifts (e.g., a political event spawning new coverage clusters). BERTopic uses UMAP dimensionality reduction followed by HDBSCAN clustering — it discovers K automatically from the data and produces far more coherent topics on short news texts because the underlying representations are BERT embeddings, not raw term frequencies.

// Rejected: LDA (requires fixed K, poor coherence on short texts), NMF (same fixed-K problem, no semantic embeddings)
WHY ABSTRACTIVE SUMMARISATION OVER EXTRACTIVE?

Extracted sentences from media articles are rarely coherent summaries — they're click-baits

Extractive methods pull the highest-scoring sentences directly from the source. In news articles, the highest-scoring sentences are often sensationalised leads that don't accurately represent the article's substance. Abstractive summarisation generates a new sentence from the full context, producing shorter, more neutral summaries that clients could feed directly into monitoring dashboards without manual editing.

// Rejected: TextRank extractive (pulls headline-biased sentences), LSA summarisation (no language generation, produces fragments)
WHY BATCH PROCESSING OVER REAL-TIME STREAM?

Media monitoring workflows are inherently latency-tolerant — throughput matters more than latency

Clients reviewed coverage reports every few hours, not second-by-second. A streaming architecture would add Kafka or Kinesis operational overhead for a latency budget clients didn't need. Batch processing let us group articles into GPU-efficient batch sizes for BERT inference, dramatically improving throughput and reducing cost per article compared to processing requests one at a time.

// Rejected: Kafka streaming pipeline (operational overhead without latency benefit), synchronous per-request inference (GPU underutilisation)
